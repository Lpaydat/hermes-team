# SaaS Benchmark Targets by Stage

Consolidated reference for evaluating a founder's numbers or building a financial model.
Targets are directional [Judgment] — they vary by ACV band, segment (SMB / mid-market / enterprise), vertical, and vintage. Use them as starting questions, not pass/fail gates.

## Retention

| Metric | Healthy target | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Gross revenue retention (GRR) | >90% (B2B) | The floor. Below this, churn is eating the business. |
| Net revenue retention (NRR) | >110% (B2B SaaS) | The single most important B2B SaaS metric. >120% is exceptional; 100% means you're shrinking without new sales. |
| Logo retention | >85% | Lower than GRR because downgrading customers still count as retained logos. |
| Sean Ellis PMF test | >40% "very disappointed" | The classic PMF signal. Below 40%, you don't have PMF yet. |

## Unit Economics

| Metric | Healthy target | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| LTV:CAC ratio | >3:1 | <3:1 = inefficient acquisition; >5:1 = under-investing in growth (leaving money on the table). |
| CAC payback period | <12 months (SMB), <18 months (mid-market), <24 months (enterprise) | Longer = more cash tied up before profitability. |
| Gross margin | >70% (software), 30-50% (services/hardware) | Services-heavy models cap valuation multiples. |
| Magic number | >0.75 (healthy), >1.0 (excellent) | (Net new ARR × 4) ÷ prior-period S&M spend. Measures sales efficiency. |
| Rule of 40 | ≥40% (growth rate % + profit margin %) | The scaling-company health bar. At early stage growth dominates; at scale margin matters more. |

## Growth (by ARR stage)

[Judgment] These are rough consensus ranges from public SaaS company analysis and VC benchmark studies (Bessemer, OpenView, KeyBanc). A private company at the same ARR stage often grows faster than these public benchmarks because the public bar to list is higher.

| ARR | Healthy YoY growth |
|---|---|
| < $1M | >100% (often much higher — base is small) |
| $1M–5M | 80–150% |
| $5M–15M | 60–100% |
| $15M–50M | 40–70% |
| $50M–100M | 30–50% |
| > $100M | 20–40% |

- T2D3 rule (Neeraj Agrawal): Triple, triple, double, double, double — the growth trajectory of top-tier SaaS companies from $1M to $100M ARR. Reaching $2M → $3M (triple), then $3M → $9M (triple), then doubling to $18M, $36M, $72M. It's a benchmark for the elite, not the median.

## Valuation Multiples (ARR × multiple)

[Speculation] Multiples swing wildly with market conditions. These are approximate ranges for context-setting, not pricing advice.

| Stage / profile | Typical ARR multiple |
|---|---|
| Top-decile growth SaaS (public) | 10–20x+ (peak 2021 exceeded this; 2022-2024 compressed to 5–10x) |
| Average public SaaS | 5–10x |
| Private growth-stage (Series C+) | 5–15x (highly market-dependent) |
| Early-stage (pre-revenue to $1M ARR) | Priced on narrative + team + market, not multiples |

## Burn & Runway

| Metric | Target | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Runway | >12 months at all times; >18 months ideal | Under 12 months = fundraising pressure; under 6 months = existential. |
| Burn multiple (David Sacks) | <1.0 (excellent), <1.5 (good), >2.0 (concerning) | Net burn ÷ net new ARR. How much cash you burn per $1 of new ARR. |
| Default alive vs default dead | Know which you are | If you stopped raising, would your current trajectory reach profitability on existing cash? Most early startups are default dead — that's fine pre-PMF, fatal post-PMF. |

## Fundraising Round Sizes & Dilution

| Round | Typical raise | Typical dilution | What it funds |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-seed | $500K–$2M | 5–10% | Product + initial user signals |
| Seed | $2M–$5M | 15–25% | PMF signals + repeatable acquisition |
| Series A | $8M–$15M | 20–25% | Repeatable GTM engine |
| Series B | $15M–$30M+ | 15–25% | Scaling a proven model |
| Series C+ | $30M–$100M+ | 10–20% | Market expansion, M&A, path to IPO |

- Target post-money ownership for founders after Series B: 50%+. Below that, you've over-diluted or raised too much too early.

## How to Use These

1. When a founder cites a metric, check it against the benchmark for their stage and segment.
2. When a metric is off-benchmark, don't just say "it's bad" — diagnose WHY (wrong segment? too early? wrong sales motion?).
3. Always ask for the numerator and denominator, not just the ratio. A 5:1 LTV:CAC looks great until you learn CAC is $50 and LTV is $250 (tiny deal size, no moat).
4. Treat any number a founder reports with healthy skepticism until you see how it's calculated. Definitions vary wildly (especially NRR, churn, and "active users").
